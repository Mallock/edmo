A companion tool for Elite Dangerous commanders

A mission HUD
with a voice.

A small always-on-top panel that reads your Player Journal live: mission cards with objectives and timers, a proactive operator that nudges you when you stall, an AI wingman answering over local neural voices, trade and exobiology leads, a space-opera chronicle written from what you actually did, and a long-term memory that remembers your factions, records and close calls across sessions. New in 0.3: it now watches your ship in real time — fuel, heat, shields, interdictions, your docking pad — calls out the hazards out loud, checks whether your hold and cabins can even carry a job before you fly it, and understands far more of the mission board (donations, scans, hacks, sabotage, smuggling and on-foot work). You can even talk to it — hold a key, speak, and it answers out loud, remembering the conversation. Everything runs on your machine.

01

What it does

module manifest
A

Live mission tracking

Reads the game's own journal files as you play. Cards show category, destination, reward, kill and cargo counters, gift commodities for VIPs, and a synthesized objective checklist the game never gives you — with live expiry timers. It now reads the whole board: couriers and cargo, passengers and sightseeing, massacres and assassinations, plus donations, scans, hacks, sabotage, smuggling and on-foot (Odyssey) work — each with its own checklist and tailored advice.

B

Proactive heartbeat

The operator notices when you stall: docked too long with hand-ins waiting, parked in a kill system without engaging, a contract about to expire. It tells you — out loud — and names the actual RES site or Nav Beacon it detected in-system. It keeps a miner company too: a greeting when you drop onto a ring, "that one's worth the limpets" on rich rocks, and tonnage milestones as the refinery fills — quietly, on strict cooldowns.

C

AI operator (optional)

Hook up the free LM Studio and ask anything — "what should I do first?" gets an answer grounded in your real missions, system intel and expiry order, and it can read your live game data on the fly to answer for certain (see below). A built-in advisor reads your PC's RAM and GPU and tells you which models actually fit.

D

Offline neural voices

Two bundled Piper voices speak briefings, warnings and stories — no cloud, ever. Six more voices are a one-click download away. Text never leaves your machine.

E

Trade & exobio leads

Every commodities market you open is remembered; when a buy-low / sell-high spread clears your threshold, a dismissible trade lead card appears. Same for planets with bio signals you haven't sampled — unclaimed Vista Genomics money, tracked. An opt-in route planner (community data via Spansh) finds profitable multi-hop runs from your current station, sized to your actual cargo hold — and keeps the next waypoint on your clipboard: jump in, and the following system is already copied for a quick Ctrl+V in the galaxy map.

F

The Saga

When you log off, the operator narrates your session as a numbered space-opera episode — real events, real credits, real names, with continuity from the previous chapter and a cliffhanger for the next. Plus in-flight scuttlebutt about your passengers and the places on your route.

G

A memory (new in 0.2)

The operator remembers you across sessions: your history with every faction, every system you've visited (and where you lost a ship), personal records — richest contract, biggest bounty, best session. It brings memories up at the right moment ("that's your best session ever") and stays quiet the rest of the time — strict cooldowns make chatter-flooding impossible. On its first run it reads your recent journals, so it knows you from day one.

H

Eyes (optional, off by default)

With a vision-capable model in LM Studio, the operator can occasionally glance at your screen and understand what you're doing — even in situations the journal doesn't record. In copilot mode it talks about what it sees — your outfitting session, the ring you're prospecting, the pad you're lining up on — woven together with your real missions, and paced so it never floods you; danger callouts stay instant. The screenshot travels only to your own machine's LM endpoint and is never saved.

I

Ears (optional, off by default)

Talk to it. Hold CtrlShiftSpace — even with the game in focus — speak, release, and the operator answers out loud. Speech recognition runs on a local Whisper model (a one-time download on your click); your voice never leaves the machine. And it holds the thread: "and how far is that?" works, because the operator remembers what it just told you — including its own stories and warnings.

J

Flight safety & ship status (new in 0.3)

The operator now watches the game's real-time status feed. A compact strip shows your fuel gauge and live hazards, and it speaks the ones that matter: "interdiction — submit and boost", overheating, shields down, low fuel. It reads out your docking pad number the instant control clears you, warns before a jump when the next star can't refuel you, and knows when you're deep in a menu so it won't nag you for "stalling" while you plan a route.

K

Knows your ship, mats & surveys (new in 0.3)

From your loadout it does the check the mission board never does: "that delivery won't fit your hold", "you don't have cabins for these VIPs", "you're not rigged to mine this". It keeps a running tally of your engineering materials and unlocked engineers, and while you explore it flags the bodies actually worth mapping — Earth-likes, water and ammonia worlds, terraformables — with an estimate of the survey data riding in your hold.

L

Reads your live data on the fly (new in 0.3)

Ask "what's worth buying here?" and the operator doesn't guess — it reads the live commodities market in front of you before it answers. With a tool-calling model it can reach for exactly what a question needs: the current market, where to buy or sell a commodity across the stations you've visited, a fresh Spansh route, whether cargo fits your hold, your missions, materials or system intel. No more being sent to buy something that sold out hours ago — and if your model can't call tools, it still answers grounded in the market it can see. All local; nothing leaves your machine.

Installation — step by step

novice friendly

Download the installer

Click the download button above — you get a single file, ED-Mission-Operator-0.3.0-setup.exe (about 131 MB — it carries the offline voices). Your browser may ask where to save it; Downloads is fine.

Run it — and get past the blue warning

Double-click the downloaded file. Windows will very likely show a blue "Windows protected your PC" screen. That's SmartScreen being cautious about a small hobby app that isn't code-signed — not a virus detection.

SmartScreen — what to click Click "More info" (small text, middle of the dialog) → then the "Run anyway" button appears. Click it.

The installer needs no choices and no administrator password — it installs only for your user account and starts the app when done.

Meet the HUD

A small dark panel appears and floats above other windows — that's the operator. Drag it by its title bar to wherever suits your cockpit. It reads "Journal connected" at the bottom when it has found your game files (automatic — no setup needed if Elite has ever run on this PC).

Start Elite Dangerous — in Borderless mode

In the game: Options → Graphics → Display → Borderless (a true Fullscreen display hides all overlays — this is a Windows rule, not the app's). Your active missions appear on the HUD by themselves; accept a new one at any mission board and the operator will introduce it, out loud.

Optional — give the operator a brain (the AI)

Everything above works without this. For AI answers and richer stories, install the free LM Studio from lmstudio.ai, then inside LM Studio:

  • Open the Discover (magnifying glass) tab and download a model:
  • Modest PC / 4–6 GB graphics card → Gemma 4 E4B
  • Gaming PC / 8–16 GB graphics card → Gemma 4 12B QAT
  • Load the model (top bar), and you're done — the default server settings are correct.

The HUD's LM dot turns green when it finds LM Studio. Unsure what your PC can run? Open the HUD's Settings ⚙ — it reads your RAM and graphics card and marks every model ✓ fits or ⚠ too big.

Voices & taste

The operator speaks out of the box (Alba, a Scottish neural voice — fully offline). In Settings ⚙ you can switch to the bundled male voice, download six more with one click, adjust rate and volume, tune how chatty the storytelling is — or mute everything with the 🔊 button.

Want to talk back? Tick Voice input in the same Voice section and grab the one-time speech-recognition download (~150 MB, fully local). Then hold CtrlShiftSpace — even mid-flight — speak, release, and the operator answers.

If you ever want it gone

Windows Settings → Apps → Installed apps → ED Mission Operator → Uninstall. It leaves your game and its files untouched (it never wrote to them in the first place).

Privacy & fair play

flight rules

● What it will never do

  • No cloud, no account, no telemetry. The only network traffic is to LM Studio on your own machine (127.0.0.1) — plus, only if you switch them on: voice downloads you click yourself and the opt-in Spansh route planner (off by default; it sends just your current system name).
  • Memory stays home. The operator's long-term memory is one local JSON file you can open, inspect, or wipe with one click ("Forget everything"). Screen glances are off by default; when enabled, screenshots go only to your local LM endpoint and are never written to disk.
  • Your voice stays home. Push-to-talk is off by default; when enabled, speech is transcribed by a local Whisper model on your own CPU. No cloud speech service, ever — the microphone is only live while you hold the key.
  • Open source. Don't take any of this on faith — the full source is on GitHub (MIT license): the tested TypeScript mission engine, the Rust shell, the prompts, everything. Build it yourself with Node + Rust, or audit the network code — it all lives in two files.
  • Read-only. It reads the journal text files Frontier writes for third-party tools; it never writes to the game's folders.
  • No game hooks. It never touches game memory, injects code, or talks to game servers — the same mechanism trusted community tools have used for years.
  • Your words stay home. The bundled voices synthesize speech locally; nothing you read or hear leaves your PC.

Does it need the internet?

No. Missions, voices, leads and stories all work offline. Internet is only touched if you install LM Studio models, download extra voices, or enable the optional Spansh route planner — each on your explicit click, all off by default.

Will it cost me frames?

The HUD itself is negligible. AI answers use your GPU for a few seconds when generated — and the built-in advisor deliberately recommends models that leave the game's share of the graphics card alone.

Which game modes?

Open, private groups and solo — it only reads what the game writes locally, so it works identically everywhere, including Horizons and Odyssey, on foot or in a ring.

Something broke?

The HUD degrades politely: no LM Studio → deterministic guidance; no voice → text; no game running → it waits. Restart the app and it rebuilds everything from the journal.
